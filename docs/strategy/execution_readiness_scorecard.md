# Decision Studio — Execution Readiness Scorecard

**Last Updated:** 2026-06-02  
**Status:** Field-ready  
**Purpose:** Deal qualification criteria to assess organizational risk for Execution Readiness  

> Because Decision Studio’s compounding moat (and renewal trigger) relies on the Value Assurance (VA) agent proving that an action generated ROI, **Execution Readiness is just as important as Data Readiness.** If an organization is too siloed, bureaucratic, or burned-out to implement AI recommendations, the platform will report $0 of impact, leading to churn.

Use this 5-pillar scorecard during the first or second discovery call. Grade prospects on a scale of 1–3 for each pillar. A prospect scoring below a 10/15 is a high-churn risk and should not be prioritized for an early pilot.

---

## The 5 "C"s of Execution Readiness

### 1. Clout (Executive Authority)
Decision Studio bridges finance and operations. If your champion can only see the numbers but cannot force operational change, the solutions will die in the "APPROVED" phase.
*   **Ideal (3):** The buyer is a CFO with a strong partnership with the COO, or a PE Operating Partner with mandate authority over the portfolio company.
*   **Risk (1):** The buyer is a Director of FP&A or Head of Data who wants a cool AI tool but has zero authority to tell the VP of Sales to change pricing.
*   **Discovery Question:** *"If the platform finds a $400K margin bleed in your B2B distribution channel tomorrow, whose permission do you need to actually change the pricing strategy, and how hard is it to get?"*

### 2. Cadence (Historical Agility)
Your Value Assurance agent tracks outcomes on 30/60/90-day horizons. If the company takes 6 months to approve and implement a basic operational tweak, your platform's 90-day ROI reports will always look flat.
*   **Ideal (3):** They operate on weekly or monthly sprints and can deploy pricing, marketing, or supply chain adjustments in days.
*   **Risk (1):** They are paralyzed by committee consensus, or their core systems (like SAP) are so rigidly locked down by IT that operational changes take quarters.
*   **Discovery Question:** *"Walk me through the last time you discovered a negative variance on your dashboard. From the day you saw the drop to the day the fix was live in the market, how many days elapsed?"*

### 3. Capacity (Middle-Management Bandwidth)
Decision Studio makes execution legible, but humans still have to do the work. If middle-management is drowning in firefighting, they will ignore the Executive Briefing emails.
*   **Ideal (3):** The company has a competent middle-management layer, a PMO, or the Fractional CFO is bringing their own analyst/implementation bandwidth to push changes through.
*   **Risk (1):** The organization is severely understaffed, running on "hero culture," and operators are actively hostile to "more tasks from corporate."
*   **Discovery Question:** *"If we hand your VP of Operations a board-ready playbook to recover $500K in COGS next week, does their team have the bandwidth to actually execute it, or are they tapped out?"*

### 4. Culture (Data-to-Action Maturity)
You are selling *counterfactual ROI attribution*. You want a culture that actually cares about whether their decisions worked, rather than just "checking the box" that a decision was made.
*   **Ideal (3):** The CFO is frustrated because they feel the company makes decisions but never goes back to measure if they succeeded. (They actively *want* the VA agent).
*   **Risk (1):** Leadership relies entirely on gut instinct and views structured data analysis as a threat to their authority.
*   **Discovery Question:** *"When you make a strategic pivot today, how do you mathematically prove to the board 90 days later that your intervention worked, versus just riding a market tailwind?"* (Hint: They won't have an answer, which sets up your Value Assurance pitch perfectly).

### 5. Cross-Functional Trust
Root-cause analysis (DA) will often point fingers. It might show that Finance's margin target was missed because Sales discounted heavily to hit volume targets.
*   **Ideal (3):** The C-suite trusts the data warehouse as the single source of truth.
*   **Risk (1):** High political toxicity. Sales has their own spreadsheet, Finance has their own spreadsheet, and they spend executive meetings arguing about whose numbers are right. (If they don't trust the data, they won't trust the AI).
*   **Discovery Question:** *"When a metric misses target, how much of the executive meeting is spent arguing about whose data is correct versus arguing about what to do next?"*

## How to Use This in the Sales Process

Do not make the prospect feel interrogated. Weave these questions into standard discovery to position yourself as an expert who cares about their success, not just selling software.

### The "Walk Away" Pivot (If they fail qualification)
If you realize during the call that they are deeply siloed and slow-moving, use it to build immense credibility by disqualifying them politely:

> *"I'll be honest with you. Our platform will absolutely find your margin leaks within 5 days. But our software doesn't fix them; your team does. Based on what you've shared about your 6-month consensus cycles, I am worried you'll pay us for brilliant recommendations that your team won't be allowed to execute. You might need an organizational alignment engagement before you are ready for a high-velocity decision engine."*

Nothing makes an executive want to buy your product more than telling them they might not be agile enough to handle it.
