## Section 1: Framework

This document serves as the master corpus for all external communications. Strategy documents are the source material—not the end product. The workflow follows this model:

1. Strategy documents (14 files) define the complete business context, market opportunity, competitive position, and product roadmap
2. NotebookLM ingests audience-specific subsets of this corpus
3. For each audience profile, NotebookLM generates tailored content (posts, emails, briefs, scripts)
4. Content is published once to the appropriate channel/forum—no one-size-fits-all documents

This mirrors how Decision Studio itself works: Principal Profiles tell the platform how to frame insights for different executives (CFO sees margin framing, COO sees operational framing). Communications strategy applies the same model to external audiences. Each audience has a profile that defines their decision context, what they care about, what's noise, and their preferred format.

## Section 2: Audience Profiles

### Profile 1: Prospect
**Who they are:** Mid-market VP Finance, CFO, or Chief Operating Officer at a $50M–$500M revenue company. Never engaged with Decision Studio. May have heard the name or been referred by a peer. KPI monitoring exists in Excel or dashboards; root-cause analysis is manual.

**Stage in relationship:** Awareness → Consideration. Evaluating whether a platform can solve a specific KPI problem (margin variance, cost overrun, revenue forecast gap).

**Key questions they're answering:**
- Does this solve my specific problem? (e.g., "Why is margin down 200 bps?")
- Can I trust a startup with production data?
- What does implementation look like? How much work is it for my team?
- What does it cost?
- How quickly will I see value?

**Trusted signals:**
- Live demo on their industry's data (not sandbox examples)
- Case study from a peer or competitor in their space
- Specific, quantified outcome ("identified $2.3M in cost savings within 30 days")
- Clear timeline to first insight (5 days)
- Enterprise security checklist
- Reference customer willing to speak

**What is noise:**
- Architecture diagrams, agent inventory, technical moat details
- Startup fundraising plans or exit strategy
- Competitive positioning language (vs. dashboards, vs. consultants)
- Roadmap features not yet live
- Founding story or founder background (unless famous/credible)

**Preferred format:**
- LinkedIn posts (short, outcome-led, clickable)
- Landing page copy (clear problem/solution/proof)
- Cold outreach email (personalized, specific to their KPI)
- Demo walk-through video (3–5 min, on real data)
- 1-pager (problem, approach, timeline, cost)

**Distribution channel:**
- LinkedIn direct messages and posts (warm network)
- Cold outreach email (warm intro preferred)
- trydecisionstudio.com (landing page discovery)
- Peer referral (word-of-mouth)
- Industry forums (CFO communities, finance tech groups)

**Tone:**
- Problem-first, outcome-led
- Jargon-free (no "agentic architecture," no "anomaly detection models")
- Confident without overselling
- Peer voice, not salesy

### Profile 2: Active Customer / Pilot
**Who they are:** Executive at a company that has signed a pilot or early contract with Decision Studio. Typically a CFO or VP Finance. Has committed budget and executive visibility. Now focused on successful onboarding and achieving quick wins.

**Stage in relationship:** Onboarding → Ramp. First 30–90 days. Success is visible progress (first situation cards, recognized KPI insights, team adoption).

**Key questions they're answering:**
- How do I get my first situation card? What's the process?
- Which KPIs should I onboard first for maximum early impact?
- What does "good" look like at 30, 60, 90 days?
- What's on the roadmap? What's coming next?
- Am I on track?

**Trusted signals:**
- Step-by-step onboarding guide with expected outcomes at each stage
- Specific examples from their industry or company type
- Visible progress checkpoints (KPI count, first assessment run, first VA decision tracked)
- Response time from support team
- Success stories from similar companies at similar stage
- Clear escalation path if something breaks

**What is noise:**
- Competitive positioning ("better than dashboards")
- Fundraising updates or Series A announcements
- Long-term product vision (show only what ships in next 60 days)
- Technical roadmap details
- Other customers' data or results

**Preferred format:**
- Onboarding guide with milestones (email-friendly, printed-friendly)
- Email sequences (triggered by milestones: KPI registered, first assessment run, first decision tracked)
- Short video walkthroughs (2–3 min each: "How to register a KPI," "How to run an assessment," "How to track a decision")
- Success story template (how to showcase their early win)
- Weekly/bi-weekly progress email (we hit X milestone, here's what's next)

**Distribution channel:**
- Email (primary)
- In-product messaging (secondary)
- Shared workspace (Notion, Google Docs, or wiki for company-specific docs)
- 1:1 calls (for blockers or escalations)

**Tone:**
- Practical, milestone-focused, reassuring
- Partnership framing ("we're building this together," not "you're trying our product")
- Transparent about dependencies (e.g., "your data is the blocker, not us")
- Celebratory on wins

### Profile 3: Partner

Partners are divided into three tiers with very different timelines and value propositions. Communications must be tier-aware.

#### Tier 0 (Now — Year 1): Data & Analytics Implementation Partners
**Who they are:** Boutique data and analytics consultancies, ERP implementation partners (SAP, Oracle, NetSuite shops), and independent data engineers who serve the same mid-market ICP ($50M–$500M). They know the client's data, systems, and gaps. They are often engaged for a readiness assessment or data infrastructure project before or alongside a Decision Studio pilot.

**Stage in relationship:** Referral pipeline. They conduct a data readiness assessment, identify clients whose KPI data is structured and accessible, and refer those clients to Decision Studio — or co-deliver once the client is ready.

**Key questions they're answering:**
- Does my client have the data maturity to use Decision Studio? (What does "ready" look like?)
- What's the commercial model if I refer a client or co-deliver the onboarding?
- Will this extend my engagement or end it?
- What does the readiness assessment process look like — can I run it as my own service?

**Trusted signals:**
- Clear data readiness criteria (what data, what format, what access is needed before Decision Studio can run)
- Referral fee or co-delivery commercial model
- Fast onboarding proof (5 days to first situation card — their client sees value quickly, which reflects well on the partner)
- Technical docs they can use (connector specs, data product contract format, KPI registry schema) — they care about architecture, unlike other audiences
- Proof that their existing implementation work is not being undone (Decision Studio reads from the same data warehouse they just built)

**What is noise:**
- MBB partnership strategy, branded agents, methodology encoding — not relevant at this tier
- Exit strategy or fundraising plans
- Long-term platform roadmap beyond 6 months
- Competitive positioning vs. BI tools (they already know the BI landscape)

**Preferred format:**
- Data readiness assessment guide (what to check, what's required, what's a blocker)
- Technical integration brief (connectors supported, data product contract format, KPI registry)
- Partner brief (1 page: commercial model, referral process, co-delivery option)
- Co-onboarding runbook (step-by-step for partner + Decision Studio to onboard a client together)

**Distribution channel:**
- 1:1 meeting (warm intro via shared client or LinkedIn)
- LinkedIn (thought leadership on data readiness for decision intelligence)
- Industry forums (data engineering communities, ERP implementation networks)

**Tone:**
- Peer technical credibility (they know data; speak their language on connectors and schemas)
- Commercial and direct (clear referral model, no ambiguity)
- Additive, not competitive ("your data work makes Decision Studio possible")

#### Tier 1 (Year 2+): Mid-Tier Consulting Firms
**Who they are:** Senior practitioners at FTI Consulting, Alvarez & Marsal, Huron, or independent advisors (fractional CFOs, operations consultants). Evaluating Decision Studio for client referral, co-sell, or embedded delivery alongside their own analysis work.

**Stage in relationship:** Consideration → Integration. Deciding whether to recommend or embed Decision Studio in engagements.

**Key questions they're answering:**
- Will this enhance my client engagements or cannibalize my fees?
- What's the commercial model?
- How does it complement my methodology?
- Will my clients perceive me as adding value or just reselling?

**Trusted signals:**
- Client success story from a similar engagement
- Proof of complementarity ("Decision Studio is the continuous monitoring layer; you're the strategy layer")
- Revenue-share or referral model
- Demo on client-like data

**Preferred format:** Partner brief, co-sell playbook, commercial term sheet summary

**Distribution channel:** 1:1 meetings, LinkedIn, industry conferences

**Tone:** Peer-to-peer, commercial, mutual-value framing

---

#### Tier 2 (Year 3+): MBB and Large Consulting Firms
**Who they are:** McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Deloitte, Accenture. Potential to encode proprietary methodologies as branded agents in the Solution Finder (e.g., a "McKinsey Growth Framework" persona). This is the original branded-agent partnership vision.

**Stage in relationship:** Not yet relevant. Deferred until 10+ paying customers, proven moat, and Series A funding. MBB firms will not engage a pre-revenue startup for IP co-development.

**Communications approach:** Do not proactively reach out. Monitor for inbound interest. Revisit when 10+ customers and a credible partner story exists.

**Tone:** When the time comes — executive-level, IP-respect-forward, long-term joint value framing

### Profile 4: Investor
**Who they are:** Angel investor or seed/Series A institutional investor. Likely has a portfolio of SaaS or enterprise software companies. May specialize in fintech or decision intelligence. Not an immediate target (company is bootstrapped until $200K+ ARR), but framing needed for when conversations open.

**Stage in relationship:** Future state (target: post-$200K ARR, likely Q4 2026 or Q1 2027).

**Key questions they're answering:**
- Is the moat real? What stops a big vendor (Tableau, Palantir, Salesforce) or a funded startup from copying this?
- What's the path to $2M ARR? How many customers? What's the CAC?
- What's the exit opportunity? Who acquires Decision Studio? At what valuation?
- Is the founder credible? Can they execute?
- What are the risks? (Solo founder, early-stage product, new market)

**Trusted signals:**
- Paying customers (even if few—real revenue, not contracts)
- Production deployment with measurable uptime/usage
- ARR trajectory and cohort retention data
- Clear ICP (do they know exactly who buys?)
- Repeatable sales motion (not one-off deals)
- Defensible moat language (registry + VA corpus + calibrated profiles, not just "we have better ML")
- Clear path to $10M+ TAM within the ICP
- Founder credibility (prior exits, domain expertise, operational rigor)

**What is noise:**
- Solo-founder constraints or moonlighting context
- Technical debt or testing gaps
- Feature roadmap (unless it unlocks a new customer segment)
- Partnership pipeline (too early-stage)
- Fundraising plans from competitors (irrelevant to Decision Studio's viability)

**Preferred format:**
- 2-page narrative one-pager (problem, solution, traction, financials, opportunity, ask)
- Financial summary card (current ARR, MRR growth, customer count, churn, CAC, NRR)
- Competitive landscape brief (who else plays here, why Decision Studio is different)
- Demo recording (3–5 min on real data, real customer use case)
- Pitch deck (if progressing to formal fundraise)

**Distribution channel:**
- Warm intro email (from advisor, existing investor, or peer founder)
- Email follow-up with one-pager and demo
- 30–60 min call (deck walkthrough, Q&A)
- Reference call with board advisor or existing investor

**Tone:**
- Confident, evidence-first
- Honest about stage and constraints
- Moat-forward (emphasize defensibility, not just novelty)
- Long-term vision (we're building a category, not just a tool)

### Profile 5: Internal / Product Owner
**Who they are:** The founder. Decision maker on product, roadmap, strategy, and resource allocation.

**Stage in relationship:** All stages. Complete context holder.

**Key questions they're answering:**
- Are we building the right thing?
- Are the key risks manageable?
- What's the next 90-day priority?
- Where will we get stuck?
- What do we need to prove by end of Q3?

**Trusted signals:**
- All data points from all other profiles
- Honest risk assessment
- Competitive watch (threats and opportunities)
- Business premise challenges (what could be wrong with our assumptions?)

**What is noise:**
- Nothing—full corpus is relevant and used for decision-making

**Preferred format:**
- Business plan with scenarios
- Roadmap with risk flags
- Competitive watch with threat assessment
- Monthly business review (financials, metrics, blockers)
- Press file with messaging (for outreach, integrations, partnerships)

**Distribution channel:**
- Private docs (Notion, Google Docs, this repo)
- Quarterly strategy review
- Weekly working notes (operational cadence)

**Tone:**
- Direct and honest
- No spin (call out assumptions that might be wrong)
- Bias toward action (what do we do Monday morning?)

## Section 6: Tone & Framing Rules (Across All Audiences)

These universal rules apply regardless of audience and ensure consistent brand voice across all external content.

1. **Lead with the problem, not the product.** Every piece of content should start with a business problem ("Why is margin down 200 bps?") before introducing Decision Studio as the solution. Problems are relatable; products are forgettable.

2. **Use "Decision Studio" in all external content.** Internal codebase is Agent9; external brand is Decision Studio. No exceptions. The brand name should appear in headlines, product names, and company references.

3. **Outcomes over features.** Frame benefits as business outcomes, not technical capabilities.
   - ✅ "First insight within 5 days of onboarding; margin impact quantified within 7 days"
   - ❌ "14-agent orchestration pipeline with parallel LLM calls and change-point detection"

4. **The pipeline is SA → DA → MA → SF → VA; never describe it as a chatbot or dashboard.**
   - ✅ "Automated situation detection → root-cause analysis → solution recommendation → decision tracking"
   - ❌ "AI assistant for KPI analysis" or "executive dashboard"

5. **Proof points over claims.** Support every major claim with evidence.
   - ✅ "Production deployment is live; tracking real Lubricants KPI data; 4-day turnaround on first situation card"
   - ❌ "Industry-leading AI" or "The most advanced decision intelligence platform"

6. **Never position as replacing consultants.** Decision Studio is a complement to consulting and analysis work, not a replacement.
   - ✅ "Consulting-quality analysis, always on. Your team focuses on strategy while Decision Studio handles the continuous monitoring."
   - ❌ "Eliminate the need for expensive consultants" or "Replace your analyst team"

7. **Audit trail and human-in-the-loop are trust signals.** Every recommendation shows the reasoning (SA findings + DA analysis); humans approve before action. Mention this in every external audience piece (especially Prospect and Investor).
   - ✅ "Every recommendation includes the data, the analysis, and requires executive approval before action"
   - ❌ "Fully automated decision execution"

8. **Specificity builds credibility.** Use real examples and real numbers when possible.
   - ✅ "Identified $2.3M in cost savings through variance analysis of procurement contracts"
   - ❌ "Saves millions through intelligent automation"

9. **Avoid technical jargon in Prospect, Customer, and Partner content.** Save architecture/technical details for Internal and selective Investor conversations.
   - ❌ No: anomaly detection models, change-point detection, multi-agent orchestration, agentic workflows, registry pattern, Pydantic v2 models, async/await, MCP
   - ✅ Yes: KPI monitoring, root-cause analysis, recommendation, decision tracking, audit trail

10. **Be honest about stage and constraints.** For Investor and Internal audiences, acknowledge that this is early-stage software. No spin; build credibility through transparency.
    - ✅ "We're a 1-person team building this in the evenings; current proof of concept is production-ready but customer support is limited to async channels"
    - ❌ Avoid overselling team size or pretending product is enterprise-grade on day 1

11. **Frame Decision Studio as a category creator, not a tool.** Avoid comparisons to existing products (Tableau, Power BI, Alteryx). Instead, position as a new category: "automated decision intelligence for executives."

12. **Use first-person ("we") strategically.** For Customer and Partner content, use "we" to build partnership feeling. For Prospect cold outreach, use "I" to maintain founder credibility and relatability (single founder).
    - ✅ Customer: "We're going to build this together"
    - ✅ Prospect cold outreach: "I built Decision Studio because..."
    - ✅ Investor: "We are building..." (can use we or I; either works)
